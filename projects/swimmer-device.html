Overview

In collaboration with a team of fellow iBioMed students, I co-designed a wearable assistive device enabling visually impaired swimmers to independently navigate pool boundaries, without relying on a sighted guide or a tapper at the pool end.

The Design Challenge

Visually impaired competitive swimmers typically require a coach or guide ("tapper") to tap their shoulder or head with a pole as they approach the wall, indicating when to complete their stroke and turn. This creates a dependency that limits training autonomy. Our device aimed to replace this external signal with one the swimmer could receive themselves.

The device uses distance sensing to detect proximity to the pool wall, then delivers a non-visual signal, through mechanical (tapping and/or vibration) feedback directly to the swimmer. The enclosure had to be waterproof, hydrodynamic, and ergonomic enough to not impede swimming performance.

My Role

I led the mechanical design and enclosure development in Autodesk Inventor. Key considerations included waterproofing to required depth ratings, ergonomic fit across different body positions (strokes), and hydrodynamic shaping to minimize drag.

Key Contributions

  • Led mechanical enclosure design in Autodesk Inventor
  • Integrated waterproofing, ergonomic, and hydrodynamic requirements into single housing
  • Validated device through physical prototype assembly and functional testing
  • Collaborated with team members on sensor integration and feedback systems
