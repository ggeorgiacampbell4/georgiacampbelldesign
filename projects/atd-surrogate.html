Overview

As part of my undergraduate research position at McMaster's Injury Biomechanics Lab, I designed and prototyped a TPU–silicone composite soft-tissue surrogate for use in anthropomorphic test devices (ATDs), more commonly known as crash test dummies.

Why Surrogates Matter

ATDs are used in vehicle safety testing to predict how human bodies respond to impact forces. The soft-tissue components of these dummies must accurately simulate the mechanical behaviour of human tissue. This includes how it deforms and recovers under compression and impact. Improving biofidelity (how closely a surrogate mimics real tissue) directly correlates to the predictive accuracy of crash tests.

Design & Fabrication

I developed the surrogate concept using a TPU–silicone composite, selected for its tunable mechanical properties. The design involved CAD modelling of interlocking geometries and custom casting molds to produce consistent specimens. Candidate material formulations were systematically evaluated, and an ASTM-based testing plan was developed to characterize compression and impact response against human tissue benchmarks.

Key Contributions

  • Designed TPU–silicone composite surrogate concept and material selection approach
  • Developed CAD for interlocking geometries and casting mold design
  • Planned ASTM-based compression and impact testing protocol
  • Conducted two 10-minute presentations to Mechanical Engineering faculty
  • Documented findings in a formalized research report
